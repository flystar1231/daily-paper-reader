# AttnRouter: Per-Category Attention Routing for Training-Free Image Editing on MMDiT 

## Guandong Li iFLYTEK leeguandon@gmail.com Mengxia Ye Aegon THTF 

# Abstract 

We study training-free image editing on Qwen-Image-Edit-2511, a 60-block multi-modal diffusion transformer (MMDiT) that concatenates noise and source-image tokens within a single attention stream. We make three contri-butions. (i) We introduce KVInject , a single-forward at-tention manipulation that α-blends source-half key/value projections into the noise-half within a localized layer/step band. KVInject is simpler than the classical two-pass Mas-aCtrl recipe and avoids the prompt-mismatch failure mode that disables MasaCtrl on MMDiT (composite score drops 31% versus baseline). (ii) We show that no single atten-tion operation dominates across edit types, motivating At-tnRouter , a per-category routing table that dispatches edits to the operation that best preserves source structure for that type. With ground-truth categories the router improves the CLIP-T +DINO-I composite by 6.4% over the editing base-line; an automatic CLIP zero-shot classifier closes 98% of this gap despite only 55% category accuracy. (iii) Through layer-, step-, and α-band ablations we localize the editing-effective attention sub-circuit: K/V injection in early denois-ing steps (S0–7) recovers nearly all of the gain of full-step injection, while injection in early (L0–15) or late (L45–60) layer bands fails to drive editing entirely; α ∈ [0 .3, 0.5] is a stable sweet spot. We also report negative results that high-light what does not transfer from the UNet folklore: simple K/V rescaling never beats baseline and aggressive variants collapse generation entirely (composite 0.084 ). We release code, pre-computed routing tables, and a 100-sample strati-fied subset of ImgEdit-Bench used in all ablations. 

# 1 Introduction 

Recent diffusion models such as Qwen-Image-Edit-2511 [ 24 ], FLUX-Kontext [ 12 ], and SD3-Edit [ 5] treat image editing as a conditional sampling problem in which the source image is encoded as additional tokens in the denoising backbone. In the multi-modal diffusion trans-former (MMDiT) backbone of Qwen-Image-Edit-2511, the source image is concatenated with the noise stream and fed through a 60-block joint attention tower. This raises a natural question for training-free editing: which attention sub-circuit drives the editing process, and can we manipu-late it without retraining? 

A long line of work on UNet-based diffusion editing manipulates self-/cross-attention to steer editing without training [ 7 , 3, 25 , 11 , 19 , 4, 10 ]. These methods rely on the architectural separation of self-attention and cross-attention in the UNet [ 22 ]. MMDiT [ 5 , 20 ] abolishes that separation: image and text tokens flow through the same joint attention, and source/noise are simply different positions within the image stream. Existing operations transplanted directly to MMDiT either underperform or fail outright; we confirm this empirically (Sec. 4.2). 

Why MMDiT changes the editing problem. On UNet diffusion models [ 8, 22 ], training-free editing has a clean lever: the source image’s self-attention activations live in dedicated self-attention layers, while text conditioning is injected via cross-attention layers. P2P [ 7] edits the cross-attention; MasaCtrl [ 3] reuses the source’s self-attention K/V; PnP [ 25 ] swaps spatial features and self-attention. This architectural decomposition gives an editor four or-thogonal knobs (where in space, when in time, which modality, which layer family). MMDiT collapses two of those knobs: source/noise/text live in one tensor, and there is no longer a self-vs-cross distinction. The editor must therefore re-derive where the editing-effective K/V projec-tions are from scratch. This paper investigates training-free editing on Qwen-Image-Edit-2511 along three axes: which operation, where in the network, and at which timestep . We focus on op-erations that act on the key/value projections of the joint attention tower, because rescaling Q has no effect on atten-tion up to a uniform scalar absorbed by softmax.

# 2 Related Work 

## 2.1 Diffusion and flow models for image gen-eration 

Diffusion models [ 8 ] learn to invert a forward noising process and, with classifier-free guidance [ 9], have be-come the standard architecture for text-to-image genera-tion. Latent diffusion [ 22 ] pushed the denoising opera-tor from pixels to a VAE latent and is the basis of ev-ery model studied in this paper. The architecture of the denoiser has steadily migrated from convolutional UNets to pure transformers (DiT [ 20 ]) and recently to MMDiT, which fuses image and text into a single joint-attention tower [ 5, 12 , 24 ]. In parallel, flow-matching training [ 15 ]replaced the score-matching objective in many recent sys-tems including SD3 [ 5 ] and Qwen-Image [ 24 ]; the choice of training objective is orthogonal to the editing problem we study. 

## 2.2 Training-free editing on UNet diffusion 

The dominant family of training-free editors manipulates at-tention activations or features cached from a source-image generation pass. Prompt-to-Prompt (P2P) [ 7] edits cross-attention maps to swap or amplify words in the conditioning prompt. Plug-and-Play (PnP) [ 25 ] additionally injects self-attention K/V and spatial features. MasaCtrl [ 3] reuses the source self-attention K/V to enforce identity in non-rigid ed-its. PnP-Inversion [ 11 ] adds three-line latent corrections on top of inversion-based pipelines. DiffEdit [ 4] uses prompt contrast to derive an edit mask. SDEdit [ 16 ] re-noises and re-denoises a guided initialization. Pix2Pix-Zero [ 19 ]steers cross-attention along learned text-embedding direc-tions. LEDITS++ [ 10 ] stacks multiple semantic edits via concept guidance. Across this family, the editing recipe always exploits the UNet’s separation of self- and cross-attention, and the source image typically lives in a dedicated inversion pass [ 17 ]. None of these methods carries over to a backbone with a single joint-attention tower without modification, motivating our re-derivation on MMDiT. 

## 2.3 Instruction-tuned editing 

A complementary line trains the editor end-to-end on synthetic edit instructions: InstructPix2Pix [ 2], Mag-icBrush [ 27 ], UltraEdit [ 29 ]. The most recent generation of MMDiT-based editors (Qwen-Image-Edit, FLUX-Kontext, SD3-Edit) belongs to this family. On DiT specifically, Edi-tID [ 14 ] explores training-free editable identity customiza-tion, demonstrating that test-time hooks on the DiT resid-ual stream can override behaviors that would otherwise require fine-tuning. Instruction-tuned editors are excellent baselines but require expensive retraining whenever a new editing capability is added; they also tend to over-trust the prompt and bleed source content (the failure mode our router addresses). 

## 2.4 Conditional generation via auxiliary net-works 

A second school adds an auxiliary network that routes a structural condition (depth, edges, pose) into the denoiser; ControlNet [ 28 ] is the canonical example. These meth-ods require training the adapter and are orthogonal to the training-free attention manipulation we study. 

## 2.5 Mechanistic analysis of diffusion atten-tion 

A growing body of work probes which attention layers carry editing-relevant signal in UNet diffusion [ 1], but very little is known about MMDiT. Concurrent work on diffusion-transformer attention guidance [ 13 ] observes that K and V projections in DiT-style backbones exhibit a “bias-delta” structure (token embeddings cluster tightly around a layer-specific bias), motivating joint manipulation of both chan-nels rather than only K; KVInject inherits this insight by always editing K and V together within the same layer band. Our layer-band and step-band ablations (Sec. 4.4, 4.5) extend this picture by mapping the editing-effective sub-circuit on Qwen-Image-Edit-2511 to layers L30–45 and steps S0–7. 

## 2.6 Routing and mixture-of-operations 

Mixture-of-experts and routing have been used at train-ing time in language and vision models [ 23 , 6 ]. To our knowledge no prior work performs per-category routing of training-free editing operations on diffusion models. At-tnRouter is closest in spirit to instance-level operation se-lection in test-time augmentation, but operates on attention manipulations rather than data transformations. 22.7 Editing benchmarks and evaluation met-rics 

For evaluation we follow the convention of measuring edit fidelity in CLIP space [ 21 ] and source preservation in DI-NOv2 space [ 18 ]. We use the recently released ImgEdit-Bench [ 26 ], which provides 561 real-image editing instruc-tions across nine edit categories. PIE-Bench [ 11 ] is the de facto UNet-era benchmark; we did not use it because its source images are biased toward Stable-Diffusion-style syn-thetic data and underrepresent the photographic distribution that MMDiT editors optimize for. 

# 3 Method 

## 3.1 Preliminaries: MMDiT joint attention 

Diffusion / flow denoising. At inference time, all editors we study sample by integrating an ODE/SDE that pushes a noise sample zT to a clean latent z0 over T steps [ 8 , 15 ]. The denoiser ϵθ (zt, t, c ) is conditioned on a text embed-ding c and, in the editing setting, on a source-image latent. Classifier-free guidance [ 9 ] produces a conditional and an unconditional forward at each step; with CFG = 4 in our setup the model executes two forward passes per denoising step. All hooks below fire on both passes. 

Joint-attention tower. Qwen-Image-Edit-2511 is a 60-block MMDiT [ 24 ]. At 1024 ×1024 resolution the image stream contains 8192 tokens: the first 4096 are noise tokens (the latent being denoised) and the next 4096 are source-image tokens (the conditioning image, encoded by the same VAE as the noise). Each transformer block contains two parallel projection sets: • Image stream: to q , to k , to v .• Text stream: add q proj , add k proj ,

add v proj .After projection, image and text tokens are concatenated along the sequence dimension and processed by one joint scaled-dot-product attention. We hook all six projections in all 60 blocks (360 hooks per forward, 720 per denoising step under CFG), recording or replacing their outputs per step. Q is never modified: rescaling Q rescales attention by a constant and is absorbed by softmax. Fig. 1 sketches the layout.

Why same-forward, not two-forward? Classical Mas-aCtrl [ 3 ] runs two forwards: a source forward with a neu-tral prompt that records K/V, and a target forward with the edit prompt that injects the cached K/V. On MMDiT, the recorded K/V are conditioned on the neutral prompt and lack edit-relevant semantics; injecting them disrupts target generation (Sec. 4.2: composite drops 31%). Our same-forward shortcut uses K/V from the source-half tokens of the edit-prompt forward, which already carry edit-relevant semantics. This trade-off is the central methodological ob-servation of the paper: in MMDiT the source tokens are co-attended with the edit prompt, so the edit semantics are baked into Ksrc even though Ksrc describes the source image, not the target. 

Cost. KVInject adds zero parameters and zero extra for-wards. The hook copies a 4096 × d slice and writes it back; on a 60-block 8192-token model this is < 2% wall-clock overhead per step. Memory cost is one tensor of shape

## 3.4 Implementation: hook system 

Our routing logic does not modify the editor’s source code. Instead, we register PyTorch forward hook son each of the six projection nn.Linear s in ev-ery MMDiT block (360 hooks total). A shared 

AttnHub object tracks the current step index (ad-vanced via diffusers’ callback on step end ) and ex-poses a replace fn:(layer, proj, step) →

Tensor callback. Ops self-gate by (layer , proj , step ) and compose by chaining (one block per op); a ComposeOps 

wrapper iterates ops in order and lets each op mutate the running tensor. This makes adding a new training-free op a 

∼30-line change with zero re-training.

## 4.5 Step-band ablation 

We split the 28 denoising steps into four contiguous 7-step bands (Tab. 4, visualized in Fig. 4). Every gain comes from 

S0–7 : injecting K/V only during the first seven steps recov-ers 99% of the gain of full-step injection (composite 0.4022 vs. 0.4028). The remaining three step bands are statistically indistinguishable from baseline. This matches the diffusion-model folklore that early denoising steps establish coarse structure while late steps refine details, and it suggests that future training-free editors can save substantial compute by restricting attention manipulation to the first quarter of the denoising trajectory.

Tab. 5 sweeps the magnitude of TextScale, which simply rescales the text-stream K/V uniformly. The behavior is monotone and capped: doubling and tripling text K/V in-creases CLIP-T modestly but trails KVInject by ∼0.6 points of composite. This confirms that on MMDiT, source-half image K/V is a strictly stronger editing lever than text K/V. 

## 4.8 Auto-router analysis 

The CLIP zero-shot classifier achieves only 55% top-1 cat-egory accuracy on ImgEdit-Bench-100. Yet the auto router (composite 0.4113) almost matches the oracle (0.4127). Why? 

Confusable categories share routes. The classifier’s main confusions are between replace /attribute /background 

(all three route to a0p3 L30 45 ), and between re-move /style (both route to a0p5 L30 45 ). 92% of misclas-sifications cross category lines that share the same route, so the routing decision is unaffected. This makes AttnRouter unusually robust to classifier weakness: the system be-haves like a 92%-accurate classifier for routing pur-poses, even though it is a 55%-accurate classifier for prediction purposes .

Anchor sentences. Each category c is described by K =5 anchor sentences (e.g., for replace : “Replace the X with Y”, “Swap the X for a Y”, ...). We embed all 5 |C| = 30 

anchors with the CLIP text encoder, average per category, and use the centroid as the class prototype. Reducing K

from 5 to 1 drops the composite by approximately 0.002 in our runs (i.e., still above KVInject-best); raising K above 5 saturates. We did not tune the anchor prompts, suggesting that the router is robust to the exact phrasing of the anchors as long as they cover the category’s surface form. 

## 4.9 Per-category breakdown 

The single best KVInject variant ( α=0 .3, L30–45) is not the best for every category: it is best for replace , attribute , and 

background ; remove and style prefer α=0 .5; add prefers the un-modified baseline (any source bias suppresses the new content the user wants to insert). Per-category gains (Fig. 6) range from +24% on style to ≈ 0% on add ; the router codifies this preference into a discrete table. 

## 4.10 Failure analysis 

We inspected all 100 edits manually. Three failure modes emerge. 

(F1) Add edits with sparse subjects. add edits ask the model to insert a new object that is not present in the source. Any KVInject with α > 0 partially overwrites the noise stream’s K/V with source K/V, biasing attention toward what is present and away from what should be added .The router handles this by mapping add to baseline, but baseline itself sometimes fails to produce the new object. This failure mode is upstream of routing. 

(F2) Style edits that demand whole-image change. On global style transfer (e.g., “make it a watercolor painting”) the L30–45/ α=0 .5 recipe sometimes preserves too much of the original brushwork. We attribute this to over-aggressive source preservation; a finer-grained mask-based op (similar to DiffEdit [4]) is plausible future work. 

(F3) Identity drift on multi-subject scenes. When the source contains two or more salient subjects, KVIn-ject’s blanket per-position blend mixes their K/V uniformly; the model then drifts identity across subjects. A position-aware variant of KVInject (apply only inside the edit region) would address this.

# 5 Discussion 

What MMDiT changes about editing. On UNet dif-fusion, source preservation is a property of self-attention reuse, and edit fidelity is a property of cross-attention ma-nipulation; the two channels are architecturally orthogonal. On MMDiT they are entangled in a single joint-attention tensor, and the editor’s only knob is which positions in the K/V tensor at which depth at which step to manipulate. This paper quantifies that knob: the editing-effective sub-circuit on Qwen-Image-Edit-2511 is the source-half K/V at depths L30–45 during steps S0–7, with α ∈ [0 .3, 0.5] . The knob is narrow (mid-depth, early-step) but controllable. 

Why does the per-category preference exist? The cat-egories that most prefer α=0 .3 (replace, attribute, back-ground) all have a clear “foreground vs. rest” decomposi-tion in which the rest should be preserved verbatim. The categories that prefer α=0 .5 (style, remove) demand a uni-form global change in which source preservation is more disposable. Add is the outlier because its required output adds tokens that simply do not exist in the source, so any source bias is destructive. This intuition gives a recipe for porting AttnRouter to new editing backbones: build a small calibration set of ∼10 edits per intended category, sweep 

α ∈ { 0.3, 0.5, 0.7}, and pick the (α, band ) with the largest composite per category. 

CLIP-T gain is small. The headline win comes from DINO-I ( +8 .5% ); CLIP-T moves only +0 .0025 , which is within the noise floor of CLIP-ViT-L/14. A skeptical reader can frame the contribution as “the router makes the model more conservative” rather than “the router makes the edit better”. We believe the right interpretation is that on MMDiT-based editors, where source bleed is the domi-nant failure mode, source preservation is the editing prob-lem; CLIP-T saturates near baseline because the underlying model is already well-aligned to the prompt at the chosen CFG scale. 

Single backbone. We evaluate only on Qwen-Image-Edit-2511. Extending to FLUX-Kontext and SD3-Edit [ 12 , 5]would test whether the L30–45/S0–7 sub-circuit localiza-tion transfers across MMDiT instances or is specific to the Qwen weights. The recipe in the previous paragraph (small per-category sweep) is the natural way to do this without 8re-deriving everything from scratch. 

Single benchmark, single resolution. ImgEdit-Bench [26] is recent and single-resolution at 1024 × 1024 .We have not yet evaluated on PIE-Bench [ 11 ] or Mag-icBrush [ 27 ]. The 1024 × 1024 setting is convenient because the noise/source halves are both exactly 4096 

tokens; at other resolutions source start must change, and our scripts treat it as a configurable constant. 

No human evaluation. CLIP-T and DINO-I are proxies; a human study would harden the claim. We provide a qualitative grid (Fig. 7) but no MTurk-scale annotation. A small ( n=50 ) human pairwise preference study against baseline is the natural follow-up. 

Future work. Three concrete extensions. (1) Position-aware KVInject: restrict the α-blend to a region predicted from cross-attention or from a small auto-mask network, addressing F3. (2) Per-step α schedules: our results suggest a step-decaying α (high early, zero late) would match the S0–7 finding without a hard step-band cutoff. (3) Differ-entiable router: replace the discrete CLIP classifier with a small learned router head trained on edit-instruction-to-(α, band ) pairs gathered from offline sweeps; this would generalize to operations outside our six-category taxonomy. 

# 6 Conclusion 

We presented AttnRouter , a per-category routing table over training-free attention operations for image editing on MMDiT, and KVInject , a same-forward α-blend that is simpler and more robust on MMDiT than the classical two-pass MasaCtrl recipe. Layer-, step-, and α-band ablations localize the editing-effective sub-circuit to L30–45, steps S0–7, and α ∈ [0 .3, 0.5] . The auto router closes 98% of the gap to the oracle despite a 55%-accurate classifier, because confusable categories tend to share routes. The work positions training-free editing on MMDiT as a per-category routing problem rather than a search for a single dominant operation, and the negative results (MasaCtrl, simple K/V scale) explain what does not transfer from the UNet era.
