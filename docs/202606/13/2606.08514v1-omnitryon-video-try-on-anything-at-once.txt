## Abstract 

Although video virtual try-on (VVT) has achieved significant progress, existing methods still exhibit two fundamental limitations: first, they are restricted to single-garment transfer, rendering simulta-neous multi-object try-on highly impractical; second, their heavy reliance on explicit external priors (e.g., garment masks) inevitably destroys crucial physical dynamics and degrades visual quality. To bridge this gap, this paper proposes the novel Try-On Any-thing task, which aims to simultaneously transfer diverse wear-able objects onto a person in a video in a single inference pass. To support and standardize this paradigm, we introduce TryAny-Bench, a comprehensive benchmark encompassing a paired video dataset alongside a tailored evaluation protocol. Furthermore, we present OmniTryOn, an external-prior-free generative framework designed to tackle this task. Specifically, OmniTryOn employs a First Frame Wearable Cache strategy, which directly provides di-verse wearable objects for the generation process through the initial video frame. To maintain consistency, we propose the Spatiotem-porally Consistent RoPE (STC-RoPE), which inherently establishes robust spatiotemporal anchors to strictly preserve complex human motions and background dynamics. Optimized by the proposed Gradual Try-On (GTO) training strategy, our model progressively masters robust multi-object synthesis. Extensive experiments on TryAny-Bench demonstrate that OmniTryOn significantly outper-forms existing specialized video virtual try-on models and general

## 1 Introduction 

Video Virtual Try-On (VVT) has received significant attention due to its extensive applications across e-commerce industry, digital entertainment, and virtual reality. Given a source video of a per-son and a reference image of the target garment, VVT aims to seamlessly transfer the garment onto the individual while strictly preserving the spatiotemporal consistency of the background and human motions. Empowered by the remarkable visual fidelity of recent advanced video generation models [ 4 , 24 , 44 , 46 , 52 ], state-of-the-art VVT approaches [ 8, 10 , 17 , 26 , 54 ] have achieved impressive generation quality and high-fidelity garment preservation. Despite these advances, a significant gap remains between ex-isting methods and real-world applications, primarily due to two fundamental limitations. First, current approaches are inherently restricted to single-garment transfer, failing to support the simulta-neous try-on of diverse wearable objects (e.g., garments, handbags,

Second, to enforce spatial localization and structural alignment, existing frameworks heavily rely on explicit external priors (e.g., garment-agnostic masks and dense human poses) ex-tracted from a complex pipeline of auxiliary models [ 27 , 32 , 48 ]. Crucially, these rigid and often error-prone representations fail to capture complex physical dynamics. Forcing the generative process to align with them disrupts spatiotemporal coherence, inevitably leading to severe visual degradations such as background distor-tions and texture collapse, as demonstrated by the baseline artifacts in Fig. 2 (b). To bridge these gaps, we aim to develop a unified, prior-free framework capable of simultaneously transferring vari-ous wearable objects onto a person in a video in a single inference pass, a novel paradigm we term the Try-On Anything task. To this end, we first introduce TryAny-Bench , a comprehen-sive benchmark tailored for the Try-On Anything task. TryAny-Bench provides high-fidelity paired videos that extend beyond con-ventional try-on categories (i.e., garments only), simultaneously accommodating diverse customization targets such as garments, handbags, shoes, and even facial identities. Due to the absence of paired videos, existing VVT datasets [ 9, 10 , 54 ] conventionally construct training pairs by utilizing destructively masked videos as reference inputs (as illustrated in Fig. 2 (a)), which inevitably erases essential physical contexts. In contrast, by offering genuine paired reference and target videos featuring distinct wearable ob-jects while strictly preserving human motions and background dynamics, TryAny-Bench fundamentally eliminates the need for ar-tificial masking. Alongside the dataset, TryAny-Bench incorporates a specialized evaluation protocol designed to comprehensively as-sess multidimensional try-on quality, thereby establishing a robust and standardized foundation for Try-On Anything. To comprehensively tackle this task, we present OmniTryOn ,an external-prior-free generative framework. OmniTryOn achieves high-fidelity synthesis by concurrently addressing two critical re-quirements: simultaneously injecting diverse wearable objects and enforcing strict spatiotemporal consistency. To fulfill the first re-quirement, we introduce the First Frame Wearable Cache mechanism. Inspired by the in-context capabilities of DiTs [ 1 , 6, 19 ], this strat-egy formulates the initial frame of the target sequence as a unified wearable object cache, enabling fine-grained object details to con-tinuously propagate to subsequent frames via full attention [ 1 ]. Crucially, this consolidated cache design offers exceptional scala-bility, effortlessly accommodating a variable number of wearable objects. Beyond successfully injecting these object details, strictly maintaining spatiotemporal consistency without external priors poses another major challenge. To intrinsically address this, we propose the Spatiotemporally Consistent Rotary Position Embedding 

(STC-RoPE). By assigning identical 3D positional coordinates [ 39 ]across the reference and target tokens, STC-RoPE ensures the exact consistency of complex human motions and backgrounds. Finally, to mitigate the optimization difficulty of the Try-On Anything task, we employ the proposed Gradual Try-On (GTO) training strategy, which progressively extends the model’s synthesis capability from conventional garments to diverse wearable objects. ## 2.2 Video Virtual Try-On 

Inspired by the remarkable success of diffusion models in video generation [ 24 , 43 , 52 ], recent efforts [ 8, 10 , 17 , 26 , 54 , 58 ] have increasingly adapted these frameworks to the video virtual try-on (VVT) task. Pioneering methods such as ViViD [ 10 ], RealVVT [ 28 ], and Tunnel Try-on [ 50 ] employ U-Net [ 37 ] architectures to syn-thesize try-on videos while preserving spatiotemporal consistency. To achieve superior visual realism and fine-grained detail preser-vation, subsequent works [ 8, 17 , 26 , 54 , 58 ] have shifted towards Diffusion Transformer (DiT) [33] architectures. These approaches typically adopt multi-branch designs, dedicating separate branches to encode garment features and explicit prior conditions extracted from auxiliary modules (e.g., garment-agnostic masks and human poses). The DiT backbone then fuses these streams to facilitate gar-ment transfer. Other concurrent works [ 17 ] incorporate Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) to enhance the dynamic details of the garments. However, the heavy reliance on these external modules inevitably incurs significant computational overhead and complicates the inference pipeline. Moreover, they are limited to single-garment transfer per inference pass, lacking the capability to support wearable objects beyond garment. To overcome these bottlenecks, we introduce OmniTryOn, a prior-free framework that accomplishes the try-on of diverse wearable objects in a single inference pass, eliminating the need for any auxiliary modules. 

## 3 Method 

Our goal is to accomplish the Try-On Anything task, enabling the simultaneous video virtual try-on of diverse wearable objects in a single inference pass without relying on explicit external priors. To support and standardize this novel paradigm, we first introduce TryAny-Bench in Sec. 3.1, a comprehensive benchmark encompass-ing a physics-preserving paired video dataset and a specialized evaluation protocol. We then present our external-prior-free gener-ative framework, OmniTryOn, in Sec. 3.2. To achieve high-fidelity synthesis, this framework introduces the First Frame Wearable Cache mechanism to seamlessly inject fine-grained object details, alongside the Spatiotemporally Consistent Rotary Position Em-bedding (STC-RoPE) to ensure the strict consistency of complex human motions and backgrounds. Finally, in Sec. 3.3, we detail the proposed Gradual Try-On (GTO) training strategy, which progres-sively extends the model’s synthesis capability from conventional garments to diverse wearable objects. 

## 3.1 TryAny-Bench: Benchmarking Try-On Anything 

Existing VVT datasets [ 9, 10 , 17 , 54 ] lack support for multi-object try-on and fail to provide paired reference and target videos. To overcome these limitations and standardize the evaluation of Try-On Anything task, we introduce TryAny-Bench , a comprehensive benchmark specifically designed for this novel task. As illustrated in Fig. 3, TryAny-Bench consists of two core components: a physics-preserving paired video dataset and a specialized Vision Question Answering (VQA)-based evaluation protocol. 

3.1.1 TryAny-Bench Dataset Construction. To construct the core dataset of TryAny-Bench, we systematically process raw e-commerce videos to generate high-fidelity paired video samples. Each sample strictly comprises a reference video, a target video, and correspond-ing diverse wearable object images. 

Wearable Object Extraction. We first curate over 1,500 raw e-commerce videos featuring diverse wearable objects as target videos. For each raw video, we sample two anchor frames from the middle segment where the person’s full body is clearly visible. We then utilize a commercial image model, Gemini 3.1 Flash Image [ 14 ], to extract the wearable objects from these anchors and composite them onto a blank background. Crucially, we prompt the image model to intentionally perturb the scales and spatial orientations of the objects. This data augmentation strategy effectively prevents

Wearable Object Replacement. To construct the reference video, we must alter the wearables in the target video while strictly pre-serving the background and complex human motions. We first employ a state-of-the-art VVT model, MagicTryOn [ 26 ], to re-place conventional garments (e.g., tops and bottoms). However, this explicit-prior-based model inevitably introduces visual arti-facts and lacks the capability to transfer non-garment objects. To address these limitations, we sequentially feed the intermediate video into a recent video editing model, CAPYBARA [ 35 ]. This step serves a dual objective: modifying other wearable objects (e.g., handbags and shoes) and simultaneously inpainting the artifacts left by the VVT model, thereby restoring strict spatiotemporal con-sistency and visual quality. For videos with visible faces, we further apply a face-swapping model [ 38 ] to alter facial identities. This sequential process yields a high-fidelity reference video featuring entirely different wearables from the target video, fundamentally eliminating the reliance on the destructive masking prevalent in existing datasets during training. 

Fine-Grained Video Captioning. To provide accurate semantic guidance, we employ a multimodal large language model, Qwen3-VL-Instruct-8B [ 41 ], to generate fine-grained text captions for the target videos. The prompt specifically focuses on the customization targets, yielding semantic descriptions in the format: " The model with [description of face], wearing [description of clothing] and [de-scription of shoes], carrying [description of handbag], is showcasing the overall look. " Prompt details are in the supplementary material. 

Quality Control and Statistics. To ensure data quality, we metic-ulously crop out overexposed initial frames commonly found in raw e-commerce videos, strictly maintaining temporal visual con-sistency. Furthermore, we conduct rigorous manual filtering to eliminate low-quality samples caused by editing error accumula-tion across the data construction pipeline. Ultimately, this rigorous construction process yields 1,460 high-quality paired samples, es-tablishing the core dataset of TryAny-Bench with 1,243 training videos and 217 testing videos. A comprehensive comparison be-tween TryAny-Bench and existing datasets is provided in Table 1. 

3.1.2 TryAny-Bench Evaluation Protocol. While established met-rics [ 42 , 45 , 55 ] provide foundational video editing assessments, they often struggle to capture the nuanced, multidimensional require-ments of complex virtual try-on scenarios. To complement these metrics and achieve a more comprehensive evaluation, TryAny-Bench introduces a specialized Vision Question Answering (VQA)-based evaluation system tailored for the Try-On Anything task. Although recent VQA approaches [ 11 , 21 , 34 ] offer fine-grained assessments for general video editing [ 56 ], their criteria are funda-mentally not tailored to the unique physical and semantic demands of novel Try-On Anything task. Therefore, we construct a dedicated evaluation system encompassing three primary aspects: (1) Video Quality , evaluated through the sub-dimensions of Visual Fidelity (VF), Action Synchronization (AS), and Environment Stability (ES); (2) Try-On Stability , comprising Object Integrity (OI), Material Fi-delity (MF), and Scale Naturalness (SN); and (3) Physical Realism ,which includes Temporal Stability (TS), Anatomical Integrity (AI), and Dynamic Plausibility (DP). Across all dimensions, higher scores denote superior synthesis quality. Crucially, the protocol pairs these quantitative scores with explicit textual rationales to maximize in-terpretability. The detailed evaluation protocol and specific VQA prompts are provided in the supplementary material. 

## 3.2 OmniTryOn 

As illustrated in Fig. 4 (a), our OmniTryOn framework is built upon Diffusion Transformers (DiTs) [ 33 ] to accomplish the Try-On Any-thing task in a single inference pass without relying on explicit external priors. The system accepts a reference video, a wearable object image, and corresponding text descriptions as inputs. Specif-ically, the reference video is encoded into the latent space via a VAE encoder to preserve complex human motions and background dynamics. Concurrently, the wearable object image is encoded and concatenated to the noisy latent sequence as the first frame. Meanwhile, the text descriptions and the wearable object image are encoded into text and CLIP tokens to inject precise wearable object semantics into the generation process. Following multiple denoising steps within the DiT backbone, the synthesized latent is finally decoded by the VAE decoder into the target video.

As illustrated in Fig. 4 (b), we prepend the latent representation of the wearable object image to the noisy target video latents, establishing it as the first frame. 3.2.2 Spatiotemporally Consistent RoPE. Maintaining the inter-frame consistency of diverse wearable objects while strictly pre-serving complex human motions and background dynamics without explicit external priors is highly challenging. To overcome this, we propose the Spatiotemporally Consistent Rotary Position Embed-ding (STC-RoPE), which grounds the reference video, the wearable object image, and the target video within a shared spatiotemporal coordinate system to facilitate their mutual perception. DiT-based generative models typically utilize Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE) [ 39 ] to assign positional coordinates to input tokens, applying 3D RoPE (𝑡, 𝑥, 𝑦 ) for videos and 2D RoPE (𝑥, 𝑦 )

for images. This positioning fundamentally dictates the attention interactions between the reference and target tokens. To prevent spatiotemporal overlap between the reference and target tokens, previous works often introduce temporal [ 1 ] or spatial [ 40 , 47 ]biases to the coordinates of the reference tokens. However, unlike various controllable generation tasks (e.g., style transfer) explored in these works, the Try-On Anything task inherently demands that the target video strictly preserves the complex human motions and background dynamics of the reference video, differing only in the designated wearable objects. Therefore, we assign completely identical 3D RoPE (𝑡, 𝑥, 𝑦 ) to both the reference and target videos, where 𝑡 ∈ [ 0, 𝑓 − 1], 𝑥 ∈[0, ℎ − 1], and 𝑦 ∈ [ 0, 𝑤 − 1]. Here, 𝑓 , ℎ, and 𝑤 denote the temporal length, height, and width of the latent representations, respectively. Consequently, grounding all inputs in this shared spatiotemporal coordinate system establishes a strict identity mapping for the hu-man motions and backgrounds. This formulation naturally provides strictly consistent spatiotemporal anchors, enabling the model to accurately localize and transfer the fine-grained features of the wearable objects. 

## 3.3 Gradual Try-On Training Strategy 

We employ the proposed Gradual Try-On (GTO) training strat-egy to optimize our OmniTryOn, steadily extending the model’s try-on capability from conventional garments to diverse wearable objects to ultimately accomplish the Try-On Anything task. The GTO strategy is decoupled into two progressive stages. Stage 1. This stage focuses exclusively on conventional garment transfer. During this stage, the paired reference and target videos feature different garments but retain identical non-garment wear-able objects. Crucially, these specific training pairs are intrinsically

derived from the intermediate outputs of the TryAny-Bench dataset construction pipeline (Sec. 3.1). Compared to other wearable ob-jects, garments exhibit larger spatial footprints and highly com-plex non-rigid deformations (e.g., dynamic wrinkles and intricate human-garment interactions), making them the most challenging to synthesize. Therefore, Stage 1 isolates this optimization difficulty, compelling the model to concentrate entirely on robust garment transfer. Stage 2. This stage expands the training scope to support the simultaneous try-on of diverse wearable objects (e.g., handbags and shoes). Building upon the foundational try-on ability acquired in Stage 1, the model efficiently learns to align and generate these relatively semi-rigid objects while further reinforcing its garment synthesis capabilities. Through this progressive training, the frame-work ultimately achieves the robust capacity for multi-object try-on in a single inference pass. ## 4 Experiments 4.1 Experimental Settings 

4.1.1 Implementation Details. We adopt the pretrained weights from Video-As-Prompt [ 1] as the foundational model, which is fine-tuned based on Wan2.1-I2V-14B [ 43 ]. All training processes are exclusively conducted on the training set of our curated TryAny-Bench dataset. Following the proposed Gradual Try-On (GTO) train-ing strategy, the model is trained for a total of two epochs, dedi-cating one full epoch to each progressive stage. During training, each video sequence contains 49 frames, and the batch size is set to 1 per GPU. The network parameters are optimized using the AdamW [ 31 ] optimizer with a fixed learning rate of 1 × 10 −5. All experiments are executed on 8 NVIDIA H200 GPUs. 

4.1.2 Evaluation Metrics. To comprehensively assess the perfor-mance of our framework, we employ a dual evaluation system comprising established quantitative metrics and the multidimen-sional VQA-based protocol introduced in TryAny-Bench (Sec. 3.1). 

Established Metrics. Following previous VVT works [ 8, 10 , 17 ,26 ], we adopt four widely used metrics: SSIM [ 45 ], LPIPS [ 55 ], VFID 𝐼 [ 10 ], and VFID 𝑅 [10 ]. Specifically, SSIM measures the struc-tural similarity between the generated and reference videos, while LPIPS captures fine-grained perceptual differences. Furthermore, VFID 𝐼 (using the I3D [ 5] backbone) and VFID 𝑅 (using the ResNeXt [ 49 ]backbone) are utilized to simultaneously evaluate the spatial visual quality and temporal consistency of the synthesized videos. 

VQA-Based Evaluation. As detailed in Sec. 3.1, we utilize a spe-cialized VQA-based evaluation protocol to comprehensively assess the multidimensional try-on quality unique to our novel task. To ex-ecute this assessment in our experiments, we employ the advanced 

gemini-3-flash-preview [ 13 ] model as our multimodal evaluator. To ensure deterministic and reproducible scoring, the sampling temperature of the evaluator is strictly set to 0.0. 

## 4.2 Quantitative Comparison 

We evaluate our OmniTryOn against recent open-source special-ized video virtual try-on baselines (ViViD [ 10 ], CatV 2TON [ 8 ], and MagicTryOn [ 26 ]), as well as two state-of-the-art general video editing models (VACE [ 22 ] and VAP [ 1 ]), on the curated OTO test set. As reported in Table 2, OmniTryOn significantly outperforms existing VVT methods across all established metrics, demonstrating its superior capability to achieve the simultaneous try-on of diverse wearable objects while strictly ensuring the consistency of complex human motions and backgrounds. We attribute the suboptimal performance of specialized VVT baselines to three inherent limitations: first, their reliance on garment-agnostic input videos severely hinders the generation of accurate physical details and authentic visual appearances; second, they ex-perience severe spatiotemporal quality degradation stemming from error accumulation during cascaded inferences for multi-garment transfer; and finally, they possess an inherent inability to synthe-size non-garment customization targets. In contrast, our framework elegantly overcomes these bottlenecks. Specifically, the First Frame Wearable Cache strategy provides the precise conditions for diverse wearable objects in a single inference pass, while the STC-RoPE mechanism inherently establishes robust spatiotemporal anchors to strictly preserve complex human motions and backgrounds. Fur-thermore, OmniTryOn substantially surpasses both general video editing models. Notably, VAP [ 1] yields the poorest overall perfor-mance, indicating that general video editing models struggle to maintain the strict spatiotemporal consistency when it comes to the novel Try-On Anything task. To further validate the multidimensional superiority of our model beyond established metrics, we conduct a comprehensive VQA-based evaluation. The fine-grained assessment results across all sub-dimensions are visualized in the radar chart presented in Fig. 5. As visually evident, the area enclosed by OmniTryOn comprehensively envelops those of all baselines, demonstrating absolute multidimen-sional dominance. ## 4.3 Qualitative Comparison 

Figure 6 presents a comprehensive visual comparison between our OmniTryOn and the state-of-the-art baselines. As visually evident, existing VVT models exhibit a fundamental functional limitation: they completely fail to synthesize non-garment wearable objects. Furthermore, when transferring conventional garments, they suf-fer from severe visual degradations. For instance, CatV 2TON [ 8](third row) produces noticeable ghosting artifacts and unnatural boundary residues stemming from destructive garment-agnostic masking. Meanwhile, ViViD [ 10 ] (fourth row) severely alters the target attributes, resulting in unintended style modifications and color deviations (e.g., hallucinating puffy sleeves in the first col-umn and aggressively changing the garment color in the second column). Similarly, general video editing models struggle to perform pre-cise localized customization while strictly preserving the original spatiotemporal dynamics. VACE [ 22 ] (fifth row) hallucinates incor-rect visual features, such as introducing arbitrary red stripes to the black pants in the second column. More critically, VAP [ 1] (sixth row) experiences catastrophic spatiotemporal collapse, completely failing to preserve the complex human motions and original back-ground dynamics (e.g., degrading into fragmented, floating layouts in the second and third columns). In contrast, our OmniTryOn elegantly overcomes all these bot-tlenecks. Empowered by our core designs, it successfully achieves the try-on of diverse wearable objects with exceptional fidelity. It meticulously preserves the high-frequency structural details of the target objects while strictly maintaining robust spatiotemporal consistency for both complex human motions and backgrounds.

## 5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we present a comprehensive solution to the novel Try-On Anything task, featuring a robust benchmark (TryAny-Bench) alongside an external-prior-free generative framework (OmniT-ryOn). To achieve multi-object customization, OmniTryOn inte-grates a First Frame Wearable Cache that simultaneously injects diverse wearable objects in a single inference pass. Furthermore, our proposed Spatiotemporally Consistent RoPE (STC-RoPE) strictly preserves complex human motions and background dynamics with-out relying on explicit external priors. Empowered by the Gradual Try-On (GTO) training strategy, the framework progressively ex-tends its synthesis capabilities from conventional garments to ar-bitrary customization targets. Extensive experiments demonstrate that OmniTryOn delivers superior visual fidelity and spatiotempo-ral stability, thereby establishing a powerful state-of-the-art stan-dard for the Try-On Anything task and paving the way for highly immersive applications such as e-commerce and digital fashion.
